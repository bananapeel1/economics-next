# Revenue, Costs and Profits — Edexcel IAL Economics

*Unit 3 (WEC13) · Last updated: 2026-04*

## Key takeaways (60 seconds)

- **Revenue** is money coming in (TR = P × Q); **cost** is money going out; **profit** is the difference. Profit maximisation is the default objective, achieved where **marginal revenue equals marginal cost (MR=MC)**.
- **Marginal revenue** equals price only in perfect competition. In every other structure, MR lies below AR because the firm must cut price to sell more.
- **Short-run costs** are shaped by the **law of diminishing marginal returns**: with at least one fixed factor, adding more variable inputs eventually raises marginal cost.
- **Long-run costs** depend on **returns to scale**; the LRAC curve falls in the presence of economies of scale, then flattens at the **minimum efficient scale**, and can rise again if diseconomies set in.
- **Economies of scale** are internal (technical, managerial, financial, purchasing, risk-bearing, marketing) and external (falling industry-wide cost as the sector expands, e.g., cluster effects).
- **Normal profit** is the opportunity-cost return that keeps a firm in the industry. Anything above normal is **supernormal profit**; below is **sub-normal profit**.
- The **shut-down rule** says a firm operates in the short run if price covers average variable cost (P ≥ AVC) and in the long run only if it covers full average cost (P ≥ AC).

## Why revenue and costs sit at the heart of Unit 3

Every market structure question ultimately resolves into a revenue and cost diagram. Whether you are explaining why a monopoly earns supernormal profit, why a perfectly competitive firm is forced to the minimum of its AC curve, or why a loss-making airline temporarily keeps flying, you are using the same underlying tools: total, average, and marginal revenue curves against total, average, and marginal cost curves. Getting these mechanics right is the single highest-yield investment for Unit 3.

Two distinctions run through this topic. The first is **short-run versus long-run**. In the short run, at least one factor of production is fixed — typically capital, land, or a fixed contract. In the long run, every factor is variable; the firm can change its scale. The second is **marginal versus average**. Marginal values (MR, MC) drive decisions at the edge — should I produce one more unit? Average values describe the typical situation across all units produced. Both matter, and the spec expects you to handle them fluently.

## Revenue

### Total, average and marginal revenue

**Total revenue (TR) is the total money received by a firm from selling its output: TR = price × quantity.** Average revenue is revenue per unit — AR = TR/Q = P. Marginal revenue is the addition to total revenue from selling one more unit — MR = ΔTR/ΔQ.

The shape of each curve depends on the market structure. In perfect competition, the firm is a price taker. It can sell any quantity at the market price, so AR = MR = P, and the demand curve faced by the individual firm is horizontal. TR rises in a straight line through the origin because each extra unit adds the same amount.

In every other structure — monopoly, monopolistic competition, oligopoly — the firm faces a downward-sloping demand curve. To sell more, it has to cut price on every unit (assuming no price discrimination). Each extra unit sold adds less to total revenue than the one before, so MR lies below AR. On a linear demand curve, MR has twice the slope of AR and cuts the x-axis exactly halfway between the origin and the point where AR meets the axis.

See Diagram: `monopoly-pricing.svg` — AR (the demand curve) and MR below it; MR = MC determines profit-maximising output; price is read off AR above that output.

### Elasticity and revenue

Marginal revenue is tied directly to price elasticity of demand. When demand is price-elastic, a price cut raises TR (MR positive). When demand is price-inelastic, a price cut reduces TR (MR negative). When demand is unit-elastic, TR is at a maximum (MR = 0). This relationship is not just diagram decoration — it explains why profit-maximising monopolists always operate on the elastic portion of their demand curve. Producing where demand is inelastic would mean MR is negative, so cutting output would raise revenue and, because cost also falls, raise profit.

The real-world example: streaming subscription platforms. When Netflix pushed through multiple price rises between 2022 and 2024 across the US and European markets, the fact that subscriber losses were modest implied the demand it faced was relatively inelastic in the short run. With MR positive and costs flat, total revenue and operating margin rose. A monopoly-adjacent firm operating on the inelastic part of its curve is leaving money on the table; Netflix moved to the elastic part.

### Revenue and firm objectives

Although the default assumption is profit maximisation, firms may target other revenue-related objectives. **Revenue maximisation** (MR = 0) is typical when managers are paid on revenue-linked contracts or when market share is prioritised, as with many loss-making tech platforms in their early years. **Sales maximisation** (AR = AC, i.e., selling as much as possible while breaking even) is sometimes claimed by supermarket chains pursuing volume growth. Amazon's long track record of prioritising revenue and market share over near-term profit — with operating margins staying low for over a decade of its growth — is the textbook illustration.

## Costs — the short run

### Fixed and variable costs

**Fixed costs (FC) do not change with output in the short run: rent, insurance, salaried staff, depreciation of existing capital.** Variable costs (VC) change with output: raw materials, wages of production workers, energy. Total cost is the sum: TC = FC + VC.

Average fixed cost (AFC = FC/Q) falls continuously as output rises — fixed cost is "spread" over more units. This is why opening a factory is so capital-intensive: the first 1,000 units carry a crushing AFC, but by the millionth unit it is negligible. Average variable cost (AVC = VC/Q) typically falls at first as a firm uses its fixed capacity more efficiently, then rises as diminishing returns set in. Average total cost (AC = TC/Q = AFC + AVC) is a U-shape that reflects these two forces.

### Marginal cost and the law of diminishing returns

**Marginal cost is the change in total cost from producing one more unit: MC = ΔTC/ΔQ.** In the short run, with at least one fixed factor, marginal cost is driven by the **law of diminishing marginal returns**. This law states that if one factor is held fixed and variable inputs are added, the marginal product of the variable input eventually falls.

The intuition is direct: imagine a café with a fixed kitchen and one additional barista hired each hour. The first extra barista makes a big difference — they can clean, serve and make coffee in parallel. The second adds a bit less; the third still less. By the fifth, everyone is getting in each other's way and the kitchen is crowded. Output still rises, but by diminishing amounts. Because the firm is paying each barista the same wage, falling marginal product means rising marginal cost.

A direct corollary: MC cuts AVC and AC at their minimum points. When marginal is below average, average falls. When marginal is above average, average rises. When they cross, average is flat — at its minimum.

### Why the short run matters

Short-run cost reasoning drives the shut-down rule. In the short run, fixed costs are sunk — they have to be paid even if output is zero. A firm facing a price below its average cost is making a loss per unit. But as long as price is above AVC, every unit contributes something towards covering fixed cost. Shutting down would mean losing everything. So the short-run shut-down rule is: produce if P ≥ AVC; shut down if P < AVC.

This is why, during the 2020 pandemic's worst phase, some European airlines continued to run near-empty flights. As long as ticket revenue covered the variable cost of fuel and crew, each flight reduced the overall loss from fixed aircraft leases — which had to be paid regardless. In the long run, that logic expires. If a firm cannot cover its full average cost, it should exit.

## Costs — the long run

### Returns to scale and the LRAC curve

In the long run, all factors of production are variable. The firm can build new factories, hire more management, take on larger leases. The relationship between inputs and output is captured by **returns to scale**. Increasing returns to scale mean that doubling all inputs more than doubles output; long-run average cost falls. Constant returns mean LRAC is flat. Decreasing returns mean doubling inputs less than doubles output; LRAC rises.

The LRAC curve is often described as the "envelope" of short-run AC curves — each short-run AC represents a given scale of operation, and LRAC traces the lowest cost at which each output level can be produced once the firm has chosen its optimal scale. The LRAC typically falls, reaches a minimum at **minimum efficient scale (MES)**, and may then flatten or rise again.

### Internal economies of scale

Internal economies of scale arise from the firm's own growth. They fall into several standard categories:

- **Technical economies.** Larger plants use more specialised equipment and benefit from the "two-thirds rule" — the capacity of a container often rises faster than its construction cost. Container shipping is the canonical example: the Evergreen and MSC "mega-ships" deployed around 2020–2024 can carry 24,000 or more twenty-foot equivalent units (TEU) and deliver per-container costs far below smaller vessels.
- **Managerial economies.** Large firms can hire specialist managers — a dedicated head of supply chain, a dedicated CFO — whose costs are spread across far more output. A small firm often has one manager handling all functions.
- **Financial economies.** Large firms raise debt and equity on better terms. A firm the size of Saudi Aramco or Apple around 2024 can borrow at a fraction of the interest rate available to a mid-sized firm.
- **Purchasing economies (bulk buying).** Walmart and Costco squeeze supplier prices because of their volume. The same mechanism allows large pharmaceutical buyers — the Indian government's generic medicine procurement, for instance — to push prices down.
- **Risk-bearing economies.** Diversified product ranges and geographic spread smooth revenue. Unilever's portfolio, from ice cream to laundry detergent, means a hit to one product category barely dents group results.
- **Marketing economies.** A single TV campaign or global platform-sponsorship deal (Qatar Airways' football sponsorships, Emirates' sporting partnerships) spreads a large fixed cost across enormous output, driving the per-unit marketing cost down.

### External economies of scale

External economies of scale arise from the growth of the industry as a whole, not from any one firm. Cluster effects are the classic example: the concentration of semiconductor design in Hsinchu, Taiwan, of fintech in London and Singapore, of chemical manufacturing in Ludwigshafen, and of coding talent in Bangalore. As the cluster grows, a shared pool of skilled labour, specialist suppliers, and logistics infrastructure emerges. Any single firm joining the cluster benefits — its costs fall because the industry has expanded around it.

The rise of Indian tech services — with firms like TCS, Infosys, and Wipro serving the global market in 2024 — is a live illustration of external economies at work. Universities have tailored courses to produce the relevant skills; specialist law firms and accountancy practices have grown to serve the sector; the physical infrastructure around Bangalore, Hyderabad, and Chennai has matured around it.

### Diseconomies of scale

Beyond some point, firms can become too large. Diseconomies of scale arise when LRAC rises with output:

- **Managerial diseconomies.** Communication breaks down; coordination gets harder; bureaucracy slows decisions. This has been a recurrent challenge for large conglomerates like General Electric historically and, more recently, for very large platforms managing vast product portfolios.
- **Motivational diseconomies.** Staff feel detached from the firm's success; monitoring becomes impractical; X-inefficiency creeps in.
- **Purchasing diseconomies.** At extreme scale, a single buyer's demand for a specialist input can push input prices up — effectively becoming a market-moving monopsony that lacks competitive alternatives.
- **Geographic and cultural diseconomies.** Multinational firms face costs from currency swings, language barriers, and different regulatory regimes that smaller rivals avoid.

The U-shape or saucer-shape of a realistic LRAC curve is what you should draw: economies, then constant returns across a range (the "flat bottom"), then diseconomies if the firm over-expands.

## Profit

### Types of profit

**Normal profit is the level of profit required to keep the firm in its current industry — the opportunity-cost return.** It is treated as a cost of production, not a residual. If a firm's next-best use of capital yields 8%, then 8% is its normal profit. Anything earned above that is **supernormal profit** (also called abnormal profit, economic profit, or pure profit). Anything below that is **sub-normal profit** — the firm is earning less than it could elsewhere, and in the long run it will exit.

A key IAL trap: accounting profit (revenue minus explicit costs) is always higher than economic profit (revenue minus explicit and implicit opportunity costs). When accountants report "Apple made $X billion of profit in 2024," that is accounting profit. Economic profit adjusts for the fact that the shareholders' capital could have been invested elsewhere. Supernormal profit in the IAL sense means economic profit above zero, not accounting profit above zero.

### Profit maximisation: MR = MC

**A firm maximises profit at the output where marginal revenue equals marginal cost.** The reasoning is mechanical. If MR > MC, producing one more unit adds more to revenue than to cost — profit rises. If MR < MC, producing one less unit reduces cost by more than revenue — profit rises. Only where MR = MC can profit not be improved by changing output.

This is the universal profit maximisation rule and it applies in every market structure. What changes is the shape of the MR curve, which depends on the structure. In perfect competition, MR = AR = P, so profit maximisation coincides with P = MC (allocative efficiency). In monopoly and oligopoly, MR < AR, so the profit-maximising price exceeds marginal cost.

See Diagram: `monopoly-pricing.svg` — MC = MR gives output Qm; price read off AR above Qm; supernormal profit = (P − AC) × Qm rectangle.

### Why firms may not maximise profit

The spec asks you to evaluate the profit-maximisation assumption. Several well-established reasons explain why firms may deviate:

- **Managerial theories.** Oliver Williamson's managerial theory argues managers maximise their own utility (salary, status, expense accounts) subject to a minimum profit constraint that keeps shareholders quiet. The divorce of ownership from control — the **principal-agent problem** — creates this gap.
- **Behavioural theories.** Herbert Simon's concept of **satisficing** holds that managers aim for satisfactory, not maximum, outcomes because gathering and processing all relevant information is too costly.
- **Multiple stakeholders.** In the long run, firms balance the interests of shareholders, employees, customers, and communities. CSR commitments, sustainability goals, and employee welfare measures can all reduce short-run profit.
- **Survival.** In a volatile environment — for instance, a low-margin airline during fuel price shocks — simply surviving and maintaining liquidity can trump profit maximisation.
- **Revenue and sales maximisation.** As discussed above, platform businesses historically pursued scale before profit — Amazon's and Meta's early years are textbook cases.

## The shut-down decision

Combining all the pieces: when should a firm stop producing?

In the **short run**, a firm should produce if price is at least equal to average variable cost. If P ≥ AVC, each unit contributes something to covering fixed costs. If P < AVC, every unit makes the loss worse — producing nothing is better. The AVC curve is therefore the firm's short-run shut-down price.

In the **long run**, all costs are variable. A firm cannot indefinitely run at a loss. The long-run shut-down rule is: exit if P < AC. A firm can tolerate short-run losses if it expects the market price to recover and cover full costs eventually — but not forever.

Real-world example: European shale gas producers in 2024 were navigating exactly this boundary. Spot prices moved through the AVC level; some producers idled wells while keeping fixed infrastructure ready to resume. This is short-run shut-down reasoning in action. When longer-term price forecasts fell below expected AC, some firms exited the play entirely.

## Exam technique and evaluation frameworks

Unit 3 questions on revenue, costs, and profit tend to fall into three shapes:

1. **Technical definition and diagram work.** "Explain how a monopolist determines output and price" — requires you to draw MC, MR, AC, AR accurately, identify MR = MC, and show the supernormal profit rectangle. Label everything.
2. **Apply to a case.** Given a scenario about a firm facing rising energy prices, falling demand, or a new competitor, analyse the effect on its cost and revenue curves and the resulting output and profit.
3. **Evaluate an objective.** "Evaluate the view that firms always seek to maximise profit." Use managerial, behavioural, and stakeholder arguments; anchor with a real-world example; deliver a judgement.

Evaluation moves that reliably earn marks:

- Distinguish short-run and long-run explicitly. A great many students apply the long-run logic to a short-run scenario and drop a level.
- Use elasticity to qualify revenue predictions. A price rise raises TR only if demand is inelastic. Do not assume.
- Use minimum efficient scale to qualify scale-up reasoning. Beyond MES, growth yields no further cost advantage.
- Bring the principal-agent problem into profit-maximisation discussions. Examiners reward this nuance.

Mark-scheme signals include: the words "opportunity cost" used correctly around normal profit; correct placement of MR and MC intersections; identification of the area of supernormal profit as the rectangle (P − AC) × Q, not a triangle; and precise distinction between variable and fixed costs in shut-down questions.

## Common mistakes

- **Drawing MR passing through the middle of the AR curve instead of twice the slope.** On a linear demand curve, MR has twice the gradient; getting this wrong invalidates the diagram.
- **Claiming normal profit is zero.** It is not — normal profit is a positive return equal to the opportunity cost of capital. It appears as zero economic profit, but accounting profit is positive.
- **Assuming economies of scale are automatic and endless.** They are neither. They depend on the industry and eventually run out at minimum efficient scale; beyond that, diseconomies can appear.
- **Mixing up MR = MC with P = MC.** MR = MC is the profit-maximising output in every structure. P = MC only happens to also be true in perfect competition.
- **Failing to distinguish short-run and long-run cost curves.** Short-run AC has a fixed factor and a U-shape driven by diminishing returns. LRAC reflects returns to scale and is typically shallower.
- **Applying the shut-down rule using total cost rather than variable cost.** In the short run, sunk fixed cost is irrelevant to the marginal decision. Use AVC.
- **Muddling diagram areas.** Supernormal profit is the rectangle (P − AC) × Q; it is not the area under the MR curve. Deadweight loss is a triangle, not a rectangle.

Use these before, during, and after reading:

- **Before reading:** Without looking at your notes, define marginal cost, marginal revenue, and explain why MR = MC is the profit-maximising rule. Then read the section and correct yourself.
- **During — explain:** Explain the law of diminishing marginal returns using a real example from outside economics (a kitchen, a construction site, a study group). Why does it eventually pull marginal cost upwards?
- **During — apply:** Apply the shut-down rule to a real case: a hotel during a low-season month. Under what conditions should it keep running rooms at a loss? Under what conditions should it close?
