# Market Structures and Contestability — Edexcel IAL Economics

*Unit 3 (WEC13) · Last updated: 2026-04*

## Key takeaways (60 seconds)

- **Market structure** is determined by four things: number of firms, ease of entry and exit, product differentiation, and how much information buyers and sellers have.
- In **perfect competition**, firms are price takers and make only normal profit in the long run, because any supernormal profit attracts new entrants until it is competed away.
- **Monopolistic competition** looks like perfect competition with branding bolted on; product differentiation gives each firm a small, downward-sloping demand curve but entry still erodes long-run profit.
- **Oligopoly** behaviour is driven by interdependence, which is why game theory and the kinked demand curve are the standard analytical tools, and why collusion (tacit or explicit) is always a temptation.
- A **pure monopoly** sets output where MR=MC and typically charges a higher price and produces a lower quantity than a competitive market, causing a deadweight welfare loss unless the firm has large economies of scale.
- **Contestable markets theory** (Baumol, 1982) argues that what disciplines a firm is not the number of rivals but the credible threat of entry; a monopoly in a perfectly contestable market behaves almost like a competitive firm.
- Judge efficiency across four dimensions — **allocative, productive, dynamic, X-efficiency** — because no structure scores highest on all four at once.

## Why market structure matters

Market structure is the single most important framework in Unit 3. Every question about a firm's pricing power, a merger, a supermarket price war, a pharmaceutical patent, or a new low-cost entrant routes back to structure. Structure shapes conduct (how firms behave) and conduct shapes performance (prices, output, innovation, welfare). This is the classic **Structure–Conduct–Performance** paradigm developed by Joe Bain and Edward Mason in the 1950s, and it still frames the spec.

The four structural variables you need to handle fluently are:

1. **Number and relative size of firms** — from many small firms to a single dominant seller.
2. **Barriers to entry and exit** — legal (patents, licences), technical (scale economies, network effects), strategic (brand loyalty, predatory pricing), and sunk-cost barriers on exit.
3. **Product differentiation** — whether products are homogeneous (wheat, iron ore) or differentiated (smartphones, trainers, streaming services).
4. **Information** — whether buyers and sellers have full, symmetric information or whether one side knows more (asymmetric information).

Put these together and you get a spectrum from perfect competition at one end to pure monopoly at the other, with monopolistic competition and oligopoly in between. The spec expects you to slot a real firm into the right structure and then use the diagram to predict price, output, profit and efficiency.

## Perfect competition

### The model and its assumptions

**Perfect competition is a theoretical benchmark in which many firms sell an identical product, there are no barriers to entry or exit, and buyers and sellers have perfect information.** No individual firm has any pricing power. Each firm is a price taker, accepting the market price as given and deciding only how much to produce.

This model is not a description of reality; it is a yardstick. When economists say a market is "competitive," they usually mean it has some features of the perfect competition model — many small players, low barriers, comparable products — rather than all of them.

The global market for wheat, long-dated government bonds, and foreign exchange trading between major currencies get closest. In agriculture, for instance, around 2024 global wheat output was shared across millions of farmers from Ukraine to Kansas to the Punjab. No single farmer can nudge the Chicago Board of Trade price; they sell at whatever the world market offers.

### Short-run and long-run equilibrium

In the short run, firms in perfect competition can make supernormal profit, normal profit, or a loss. They produce where MC=MR and, because price equals marginal revenue in perfect competition, also where P=MC. That P=MC condition is exactly what delivers allocative efficiency — the marginal cost of the last unit equals what consumers are willing to pay for it.

In the long run, supernormal profit attracts new entrants. New entrants shift market supply rightwards, dragging the market price down until the typical firm earns only normal profit. Losses work in reverse: firms exit, supply contracts, price rises, surviving firms return to normal profit. The long-run equilibrium sits at the minimum of the long-run average cost (LRAC) curve — firms are forced to be productively efficient because any firm with higher costs cannot survive at the market price.

See Diagram: `perfect-competition-lr.svg` — long-run equilibrium with P = MC = minimum AC, showing zero supernormal profit.

### Evaluation

Perfect competition gets a high score for allocative and productive efficiency in the long run. It scores badly on dynamic efficiency. With no supernormal profit to reinvest and no way to appropriate the returns to an innovation (because rivals can copy instantly), firms in pure perfect competition have a weak incentive to fund R&D. This is a key evaluation point: efficient today is not the same as efficient over time.

## Monopolistic competition

### Many firms, but differentiated products

**Monopolistic competition is a structure with many competing firms, low barriers to entry, but each firm sells a slightly differentiated product.** Think independent cafés in Kuala Lumpur, nail salons in Dubai, boutique clothing labels on Instagram, or the several dozen ride-hailing apps that exist regionally across Southeast Asia. Each seller has a little bit of pricing power because their product is not perfectly substitutable for a rival's, but rivals are close enough that demand is elastic.

Because each firm's product is unique, it faces a downward-sloping demand curve — unlike the horizontal line faced by a perfectly competitive firm. But because entry is easy, any supernormal profit invites imitators, and the demand curve shifts leftwards and becomes flatter until tangency with the LRAC curve delivers only normal profit in the long run.

### Efficiency implications

Monopolistic competition fails both allocative and productive efficiency in the long run, but only mildly. The firm produces where MR=MC, which sits to the left of minimum AC, so there is some excess capacity. Price exceeds MC, so allocative efficiency is not reached. Defenders of the model argue that consumers pay a small efficiency cost in exchange for variety — a café that serves only identical coffee at the absolute minimum cost is productively efficient but dull. Edward Chamberlin, who formalised the model in the 1930s, saw this variety as a genuine consumer benefit, not a distortion.

## Oligopoly

### The defining feature is interdependence

**An oligopoly is a market dominated by a small number of large firms whose decisions directly affect one another.** The standard quantitative signal is a high concentration ratio — for instance, a five-firm concentration ratio above around 60% usually suggests oligopolistic behaviour. Global smartphone shipments in 2024 were dominated by Samsung, Apple, and a cluster of Chinese manufacturers led by Xiaomi; global commercial aircraft by Airbus and Boeing; the UAE mobile network market by Etisalat and du.

The distinguishing feature of oligopoly is interdependence. Each firm, when setting its price or launching a new product, has to anticipate how rivals will respond. This is why we need game theory and the kinked demand curve — ordinary MR=MC reasoning applied in isolation misses the point.

### The kinked demand curve

Paul Sweezy's kinked demand curve (1939) argues that oligopolists face asymmetric reactions from rivals. If a firm cuts its price, rivals match the cut to avoid losing market share — so the price cut does not deliver the expected volume gain. If a firm raises its price, rivals do not follow, and the firm loses customers quickly. The result is a demand curve that is elastic above the current price and inelastic below it — a "kink" at the prevailing price.

Because of the kink, marginal revenue jumps discontinuously. Within this vertical gap in MR, changes in marginal cost do not change the profit-maximising output. This is the model's key prediction: oligopoly prices are sticky. You can find this pattern in fuel retailing, airline fares on competitive routes, and supermarket own-brand pricing.

See Diagram: `kinked-demand-oligopoly.svg` — demand kink at prevailing price, vertical gap in MR explains why modest cost changes leave price unchanged.

### Game theory and the prisoner's dilemma

Oligopoly pricing can also be framed as a repeated prisoner's dilemma. If two firms can either collude (charge a high price) or cheat (undercut), the dominant strategy for each is to undercut — leaving both worse off than if they had cooperated. In repeated interactions, tit-for-tat strategies can sustain cooperation, which is one reason tacit collusion is more common than one-shot deviation in real markets.

OPEC+ is the textbook real-world example. In 2023–2024, Saudi Arabia and Russia coordinated supply cuts to prop up oil prices, but were continually tempted by smaller OPEC members to over-produce beyond their quota — a live prisoner's dilemma.

### Collusion — tacit and explicit

Explicit collusion is illegal in most jurisdictions. In 2024, the European Commission continued several investigations into alleged cartels across sectors from rubber chemicals to foreign-exchange trading, with fines typically running into hundreds of millions of euros. Explicit collusion is hard to sustain because of the incentive to cheat, the risk of detection, and antitrust penalties.

Tacit collusion is more common. Without any meeting or agreement, firms watch each other and settle into parallel pricing. Price leadership — where one firm, often the largest, adjusts its price and others follow — is one mechanism. The UK competition authority investigated parallel pricing by major UK supermarkets on fuel in 2023; similar patterns have been flagged by authorities in South Africa and Australia.

### Non-price competition

Because price competition is risky, oligopolists often compete on non-price dimensions: advertising, loyalty schemes, product innovation, store location, and service quality. This is why you see streaming platforms (Netflix, Disney+, Amazon Prime, local entrants like Shahid and Viu) spending heavily on original content rather than cutting subscription prices in a straight race to the bottom.

## Monopoly

### Defining the monopoly

**A pure monopoly is a single seller with no close substitutes and significant barriers to entry.** In practice, the Edexcel definition extends to firms with more than a 25% market share (UK working definition) because such firms may be able to exercise meaningful pricing power. Microsoft's dominance of the desktop operating system market, Google Search's global share of over 90% in most regions around 2024, and the Saudi Aramco position in low-cost oil are all examples of firms that behave, at least in some dimensions, like monopolists.

### Monopoly pricing and output

A monopolist sets output where MC=MR and charges the price consumers are willing to pay at that quantity on the AR curve. Because MR lies below AR for any downward-sloping demand, the monopoly price is above MC. The consequence is allocative inefficiency: some consumers who value the product above its marginal cost are priced out. This is the deadweight welfare loss shown as the triangle between the demand curve, the marginal cost curve, and the monopoly output.

See Diagram: `monopoly-pricing.svg` — MC=MR determines output Qm, price read off AR above AC gives supernormal profit rectangle; deadweight loss triangle between Qm and the competitive quantity Qc.

### Price discrimination

If a monopolist can separate markets with different elasticities, prevent resale, and has market power, it can price-discriminate — charging different prices for essentially the same product. Airline fares are the canonical example: the business traveller booking last-minute on Emirates or Singapore Airlines pays several multiples of the leisure traveller who booked six months ahead for the same seat class. Streaming services use regional pricing for the same reason — Netflix's subscription in India around 2024 was a fraction of its US price, reflecting different price elasticities.

Price discrimination is not automatically bad. It can increase output closer to the socially efficient level and enable services (off-peak travel, discounted student tickets) that would not otherwise exist. But it also transfers consumer surplus to the firm.

### Natural monopoly

A natural monopoly arises where long-run average costs fall continuously over the entire relevant range of demand, so a single firm produces more cheaply than several competing firms. Water distribution, electricity transmission grids, and rail tracks are classic examples. In these cases, the policy challenge is not to break up the monopoly but to regulate its pricing — typically via price caps (the RPI–X model used for UK utilities from the 1980s) or rate-of-return regulation.

### Evaluation: is monopoly always bad?

Standard welfare analysis says monopoly produces less, charges more, and destroys consumer surplus relative to perfect competition. But three counterpoints matter.

First, Joseph Schumpeter argued that monopoly profit is the prize that drives innovation — without the prospect of a temporary monopoly, firms would not invest in risky R&D. The pharmaceutical industry, where patents grant temporary monopoly power on new drugs, is the clearest case. The extraordinary speed of mRNA COVID-19 vaccine development in 2020–2021 was partly a response to expected monopoly returns under patent protection.

Second, large firms can exploit economies of scale that small competitors cannot, so a monopoly's AC curve may sit below the AC of a fragmented industry. The monopoly price can be lower than the competitive price in this case.

Third, dynamic efficiency — innovation over time — may be higher under a monopoly with deep pockets than under perfect competition. This is a Unit 3 evaluation staple.

## Contestable markets

### The Baumol challenge

**A contestable market is one where entry and exit are free, entry is not sunk, and incumbents cannot retaliate quickly enough to prevent an entrant making profit.** William Baumol, John Panzar, and Robert Willig formalised contestable markets theory in the early 1980s. Their claim was radical: the number of firms in a market tells you very little about how competitive behaviour will be. What matters is the credible threat of entry.

If hit-and-run entry is possible — a new firm can enter, capture some supernormal profit, then exit without losing its investment — then even a pure monopoly will be forced to price close to average cost and keep costs efficient. The monopolist behaves competitively because it has to, not because it wants to.

### Conditions for contestability

For a market to be fully contestable, four conditions need to hold:

1. **No sunk costs on entry.** A potential entrant must be able to recover its investment on exit. Low-cost leased aircraft and drivers in ride-hailing are classic "low sunk cost" resources; bespoke chip fabrication plants are not.
2. **No legal barriers.** Licences, patents, and exclusive dealing arrangements erode contestability.
3. **Access to the same technology as incumbents.** An entrant must be able to produce at the incumbent's cost level.
4. **No strategic retaliation before the entrant recovers costs.** Limit pricing and predatory pricing reduce contestability.

### Real-world examples

Low-cost long-haul aviation from around 2015 onwards has tested contestability in practice. Norwegian's transatlantic push, followed by Play Airlines (Iceland), illustrated how entry on profitable routes could discipline pricing — but also how high sunk costs (aircraft on long-term leases, airport slots) made hit-and-run entry fragile. Several of these ventures failed or restructured between 2019 and 2024.

Digital platforms often display high contestability in principle (low physical capital) but low contestability in practice (network effects, data advantages, lock-in). WhatsApp's displacement of SMS and BBM shows contestability in action; the difficulty any rival has had in dislodging Meta's messaging dominance since then shows how quickly a contestable market can calcify.

### Why contestability is an evaluation lever

The contestable markets framework is not really a fifth market structure. It is an evaluation tool. When an exam question asks whether a monopoly will exploit consumers, the well-trained IAL student uses contestability to argue that the answer depends on whether entry is a credible threat, not just on the number of incumbents. That is the Unit 3 analytical upgrade.

## Barriers to entry and exit

Barriers to entry are the single most important reason market structures differ. Classify them as:

- **Structural barriers.** Economies of scale, high fixed costs, absolute cost advantages, network effects. TSMC's advanced-node chip fabrication plants, costing upwards of $20 billion each around 2024, are a structural barrier of awesome magnitude: no new entrant can replicate that capital scale overnight.
- **Strategic barriers.** Limit pricing, predatory pricing, heavy advertising, exclusive supplier agreements, capacity pre-emption. These are created deliberately by incumbents.
- **Legal barriers.** Patents, copyright, licensing regimes, import tariffs, regulatory approval. The five-year patent extension on some pharmaceuticals and the spectrum licensing regimes that limit mobile network operators are classic legal barriers.
- **Brand-based barriers.** Established brand loyalty and sunk advertising expenditure. Coca-Cola's global brand equity is a genuine deterrent to entry even where production technology is widely available.

Barriers to exit matter too. If a firm cannot recover its investment — because assets are industry-specific, redundancy costs are high, or long leases lock in rent — it will be slow to exit a loss-making market, and entry will be correspondingly deterred.

## Efficiency across market structures

The exam often asks you to compare efficiency across structures. Distinguish four concepts:

- **Allocative efficiency.** Price equals marginal cost (P = MC), so the last unit produced is valued by consumers at exactly what it costs society to produce. Reached in perfect competition; missed in monopolistic competition, oligopoly, and monopoly.
- **Productive efficiency.** Production at the minimum of the average cost curve. Reached in perfect competition in the long run; not reached in the others unless by accident.
- **Dynamic efficiency.** Improvement in products, processes, and cost functions over time through innovation and R&D. Depends on retained profit and appropriability — usually higher in oligopoly and monopoly than in perfect competition.
- **X-efficiency.** The absence of slack, waste, or managerial complacency. Harvey Leibenstein's concept (1966): without competitive pressure, managers slack off and AC drifts upwards. X-inefficiency is a recognised risk in monopoly and in sheltered state-owned enterprises.

No structure wins on all four. Perfect competition scores highest on allocative and productive; oligopoly and monopoly typically score highest on dynamic; X-efficiency is best disciplined by competition or by credible contestability.

## Exam technique and evaluation frameworks

Unit 3 structures questions into define/explain/analyse/evaluate ladders. The lower-tariff questions want technical definition and correct diagram work. The high-tariff questions — typically 10 or 12-mark "evaluate" — reward a sustained argument, a balanced counter, and judgement.

A reliable five-move structure for a market structure evaluation question is:

1. **Set up** with the market structure the question describes, naming the firm or industry given in the case.
2. **Analyse** the first-order effect using the right diagram (monopoly pricing, kinked demand, perfect competition LR). Be precise: identify output, price, and the area of supernormal profit.
3. **Extend** to efficiency and welfare consequences. Use all four efficiency concepts.
4. **Evaluate** with a counter-point: contestability, economies of scale, dynamic efficiency, price discrimination benefits, or elasticity-sensitive consumer impact.
5. **Judge** with a reasoned conclusion tied to the specific context — not a generic "it depends."

Command-word traps to watch for:

- "Explain" demands a developed chain of reasoning; a single sentence definition is insufficient.
- "Analyse" requires a diagram where relevant and a causal chain — show the mechanism.
- "Evaluate" requires an explicit judgement. Without a reasoned conclusion you cap at L3 on most mark schemes.
- "To what extent" questions are evaluate questions in disguise; you must settle on a position.

Mark-scheme signals examiners look for include: correct labelling on diagrams, identification of the specific area of deadweight loss or supernormal profit, use of subject-specific vocabulary (not "lots of firms" but "many buyers and sellers, each too small to influence market price"), and a context-anchored conclusion.

## Common mistakes

- **Confusing MC = MR (profit max condition) with P = MC (allocative efficiency).** These coincide in perfect competition only. In any other structure, P > MC because MR lies below AR.
- **Claiming monopolies always make supernormal profit.** A monopoly can make a loss in the short run if demand is low. What it cannot do — unlike a competitive firm — is have entry wipe out any short-run supernormal profit, because barriers to entry block that process.
- **Treating the kinked demand curve as a complete theory of oligopoly.** It explains price rigidity but not how the prevailing price was set in the first place. Pair it with game theory.
- **Assuming contestable markets theory overturns monopoly analysis.** It qualifies it. Real markets are rarely perfectly contestable; sunk costs and strategic retaliation are pervasive.
- **Diagram errors.** Forgetting that MR has twice the slope of AR on a linear demand curve; labelling the supernormal profit area using the wrong rectangle; placing the monopoly price on the MC curve rather than the AR curve.
- **Muddling internal and external economies of scale.** Internal: LRAC falls as the firm grows. External: AC falls as the industry grows.

## Retrieval practice prompts

Use these before, during, and after reading:

- **Before reading:** List every market structure you can name and one real firm or industry for each. What makes them different?
- **During — explain:** Explain in your own words why, in long-run perfect competition, the typical firm produces at the minimum of its LRAC curve and earns only normal profit.
- **During — apply:** Apply oligopoly theory to the global commercial aircraft industry (Airbus and Boeing). What does the kinked demand curve predict? What does game theory predict when there are only two buyers (Boeing's 787 versus Airbus's A350) competing for a multi-billion-dollar airline order?
- **After — connect:** Connect contestability theory to the Unit 2 policy question: does a regulator need to break up a monopoly, or is the threat of entry enough?
- **After — self-test:** Without looking at your notes, draw and label the monopoly diagram, then mark the area of supernormal profit, deadweight loss, and consumer surplus. Then do the same for perfect competition in the long run.
