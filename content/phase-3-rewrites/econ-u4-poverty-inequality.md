# Poverty and Inequality — Edexcel IAL Economics

*Unit 4 (WEC14) · Last updated: 2026-04*

## Key takeaways (60 seconds)
- **Absolute poverty** is measured against a fixed subsistence standard; **relative poverty** is measured against the income distribution of the society in which a person lives.
- The **World Bank** extreme-poverty line sits at 2.15 US dollars per day in 2017 PPP; around 700 million people lived below it in 2024, with progress having stalled since 2020.
- **Inequality** is measured through the **Lorenz curve** and the **Gini coefficient**; the further the curve sags from the line of equality, the larger the Gini and the more unequal the distribution.
- Causes include **unemployment**, **wage dispersion**, **discrimination**, **capital concentration**, and the uneven effects of **globalisation** and technological change.
- Policies fall broadly into **market-based** (education, training, land reform, financial inclusion), **redistributive** (progressive tax, welfare transfers), and **regulatory** (minimum wage, labour protection) categories.
- The **equity-efficiency trade-off** frames most exam debates; Amartya Sen, Thomas Piketty, and Banerjee and Duflo are the three theorists most worth citing.
- **Developing-country** contexts differ sharply from developed-country contexts — informal employment, subsistence agriculture, and weak tax capacity change which policies are feasible.

## Concepts and definitions

### Absolute poverty

**Absolute poverty** is a condition in which a person cannot afford the basic goods and services needed for survival — food, shelter, clothing, basic health care, and basic education. It is measured against a fixed threshold that does not shift with the rest of society's living standards.

The most widely used line is the World Bank's **international extreme-poverty line**, updated in 2022 to 2.15 US dollars per person per day in 2017 purchasing power parity terms. Higher lines of 3.65 and 6.85 US dollars per day are used for lower-middle-income and upper-middle-income country benchmarks. In 2024 the World Bank's *Poverty, Prosperity, and Planet* report estimated around 692 million people remained below the extreme line, a figure that has stalled since the pandemic knocked roughly 70 million people back into extreme poverty in 2020.

National absolute lines exist in addition to the global line. India's Rangarajan Committee line (rural roughly 972 rupees per capita per month in 2011–12 prices, updated since) and Pakistan's national poverty line serve national policy. For IAL evaluation it matters that global and national lines do not always agree — a household above the international line may still be classified poor locally.

### Relative poverty

**Relative poverty** is a condition in which a person's income or consumption is significantly below the median of their society. The European Union's standard threshold is 60% of national equivalised median income. The OECD often uses 50%. A household above the World Bank extreme line can still be relatively poor in a rich country.

Relative poverty is stubbornly high even in developed economies — around 16% in the European Union in 2024 on the 60%-of-median measure, and higher in the UK and US. It captures *exclusion* from the material standard that most people take for granted — internet access, school trips, minor travel, participation in social life.

Absolute measures matter most in low-income contexts; relative measures matter most in middle- and high-income contexts. Good IAL answers explain which matters more for the country in the question.

### Multidimensional Poverty Index (MPI)

Income alone misses deprivation in health, education, and living standards. The **Multidimensional Poverty Index**, developed by Sabina Alkire and James Foster at the Oxford Poverty and Human Development Initiative and used by UNDP since 2010, tracks ten indicators across three dimensions:

- *Health* — nutrition, child mortality.
- *Education* — years of schooling, school attendance.
- *Living standards* — cooking fuel, sanitation, drinking water, electricity, housing, assets.

The UNDP 2024 MPI report estimated around 1.1 billion people — over 18% of the population in the 112 countries assessed — lived in multidimensional poverty. The largest shares sit in sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia. Strong IAL answers note that someone can be income-non-poor and multidimensionally poor (for example, consuming enough calories but without sanitation), or vice versa.

### Human Development Index (HDI)

The **HDI**, designed by Mahbub ul Haq with Amartya Sen's guidance for the UNDP, combines three dimensions — long and healthy life (life expectancy), knowledge (mean and expected years of schooling), and a decent standard of living (GNI per capita in PPP). It is a broader *development* measure, not a poverty measure, but is strongly correlated with poverty outcomes.

The 2024 Human Development Report showed HDI recovering unevenly from pandemic losses: high-income countries near full rebound, many low-HDI countries still below 2019 levels. Norway and Switzerland top the 2024 rankings; South Sudan, Chad, and Central African Republic sit at the bottom.

### Inequality of income vs wealth

**Income** is a flow (earnings per period). **Wealth** is a stock (assets owned at a point in time). Wealth inequality is almost always higher than income inequality because wealth compounds and is inherited.

In the UK, the top 10% of households hold roughly half of total wealth; in the US, the top 10% hold closer to 70%. Emerging-market figures are often higher again — Credit Suisse / UBS Global Wealth reports through 2023–2024 put India's top-10% wealth share above 60%.

## Measuring inequality

### Lorenz curve

The **Lorenz curve** plots cumulative share of population (poorest first) on the horizontal axis against cumulative share of income or wealth on the vertical axis.

> *Diagram: `lorenz-curve.svg` — square plot with the 45-degree line of perfect equality above, the Lorenz curve bowing below it, the area between labelled A.*

Perfect equality is the 45-degree diagonal. Perfect inequality is the curve along the x-axis then up the right-hand side. Real economies fall in between. The further the curve sags below the diagonal, the more unequal the distribution.

### Gini coefficient

The **Gini coefficient** summarises the Lorenz curve in a single number. It is the ratio of the area between the curve and the line of equality to the total area under the line of equality — or equivalently, 2 × area A in the standard diagram.

- Gini = 0: perfect equality (everyone has the same income).
- Gini = 1: perfect inequality (one person has all income).

Indicative 2024 disposable-income Gini coefficients (rounded):
- South Africa: around 0.63 — one of the highest in the world, reflecting deep historical inequalities.
- Brazil: around 0.52.
- United States: around 0.40.
- United Kingdom: around 0.35.
- India: around 0.35 (consumption-based; wealth Gini is much higher).
- Saudi Arabia and UAE: not officially published; estimated roughly 0.40–0.50 including non-citizens.
- Pakistan: around 0.30 (consumption).
- Sweden and Denmark: around 0.26–0.28.

Limitations of the Gini:
- Insensitive to *where* in the distribution inequality sits — two countries with very different top-1% shares can share a Gini.
- Captures only relative inequality, not absolute levels of income.
- Depends heavily on definitions — pre-tax vs post-tax, market vs disposable income.

### Other measures

Complementary measures:
- **Top 10% / bottom 40% income ratio** (Palma ratio) — simple and stable; used by UNDP.
- **Top 1% income share** — popularised by Thomas Piketty's *Capital in the Twenty-First Century* (2014) using long-run income-tax data.
- **Inter-quintile ratios** (S80/S20) — standard in Eurostat reporting.

## Causes of poverty and inequality

### Unemployment and underemployment

Unemployment is the most direct route into income poverty in economies with limited welfare. Long spells of unemployment erode skills (hysteresis) and worsen mental health, making re-entry harder.

In many developing countries the more relevant phenomenon is **underemployment** — working fewer hours than desired or in jobs that do not use a person's skills. ILO data through 2024 show informal and underemployed workers form the majority of the labour force in South Asia and sub-Saharan Africa. Even in GCC economies such as the UAE and Saudi Arabia, where nominal unemployment is low, labour-market dualism between citizens and migrants produces its own inequalities.

### Wage dispersion and the skill premium

The gap between high-skill and low-skill wages has widened in most OECD economies since the 1980s, driven by:
- **Skill-biased technological change** — technology complements high-skill workers and substitutes for routine-task workers.
- **Globalisation** — offshoring of routine manufacturing depressed unskilled wages in developed economies.
- **Unionisation decline** — eroding the wage floor for middle-income workers in many advanced economies.

The arrival of generative AI from 2022–2024 is reshaping the picture. Early evidence from the IMF and OECD suggests AI may compress some high-skill wage premia (junior legal, junior coding, routine analysis) while favouring workers who combine judgement with AI fluency. The net effect on inequality is disputed.

### Discrimination

Labour-market discrimination on gender, ethnicity, migrant status, caste, disability, or religion produces persistent wage and employment gaps even after controlling for education. World Bank data from 2024 shows women in some Middle East and North Africa economies have participation rates 30–40 percentage points below men. India's gender gap in labour-force participation has widened, not narrowed, over the 2010s.

Recent reforms matter. Saudi Arabia's Vision 2030 labour-market reforms raised female labour-force participation from roughly 17% in 2016 to above 35% by 2023. The UAE's 2022 labour law introduced paid parental leave and anti-discrimination protections. These are illustrative in IAL answers of how policy can shift structural inequalities.

### Capital concentration and rentier incomes

Piketty's central empirical claim is that when the rate of return on capital (r) exceeds the rate of economic growth (g), wealth-to-income ratios rise and the share of income accruing to capital owners grows. He calls this tendency **r > g**.

If true, inequality widens absent offsetting policy — progressive taxation, inheritance taxes, or large-scale destruction of capital (as in the two World Wars). Critics such as Acemoglu argue the mechanism is more contingent on institutions. IAL answers that engage with r > g and its critiques are rewarded.

### Globalisation

Globalisation has a double-edged effect on inequality:
- **Between-country inequality** has narrowed since the 1990s as China, India, Vietnam, Bangladesh, and Indonesia have grown rapidly (the "convergence" story).
- **Within-country inequality** has widened in many countries — both winners (export-competitive coastal regions in China, IT hubs in India) and losers (manufacturing regions in the US and UK Midlands).

Branko Milanovic's "elephant chart" shows rising real incomes for much of the world's middle (largely Asian emerging middle class) and the top 1% globally, with relative stagnation for the lower-middle of advanced economies.

### Informal economy and weak state capacity

In developing countries, most of the poor work informally — no written contract, no social insurance, no enforceable labour rights. This insulates them from minimum-wage laws and welfare systems. The World Bank estimates informal workers make up roughly 70% of total employment in South Asia and over 85% in sub-Saharan Africa. Weak tax capacity (low tax-to-GDP ratios, often under 15% in low-income countries) limits redistributive space.

### Debt distress

Rising interest rates from 2022 pushed many low-income countries into debt distress. The IMF reported in 2024 that more than half of low-income countries were at high risk of, or in, debt distress. Debt service crowds out health, education, and infrastructure spending, feeding back into poverty and inequality. Sri Lanka's 2022 default and subsequent IMF programme is a clear recent example; Ghana, Zambia, and Pakistan have all required restructuring or programmes.

## Policies to reduce poverty and inequality

Strong IAL answers structure policies into categories, explain the mechanism, and evaluate both intended effects and unintended consequences.

### Progressive taxation

A **progressive tax** takes a larger proportion of income from higher earners. Income tax, capital gains tax, inheritance tax, and wealth tax are standard instruments.

The 2024 implementation of the **OECD/G20 Pillar Two global minimum corporate tax** — a 15% minimum effective rate, now active in around 55 jurisdictions including the EU, UK, Japan, Korea, and Canada — is a rare example of international coordination aimed at redistribution. Its sponsors argue it limits tax competition that erodes national tax bases. Critics argue the 15% rate is low, exemptions are wide, and the benefits accrue disproportionately to already-wealthy residence-country treasuries.

Evaluation: progressive taxes can reduce post-tax inequality, but aggressive rates may reduce work incentives, encourage avoidance, and provoke capital flight. Empirical evidence (Saez and Zucman for the US; IFS and Resolution Foundation for the UK) suggests moderate progressivity causes little disincentive; effects rise steeply at very high marginal rates.

### Welfare and social protection

Cash transfers, unemployment insurance, disability benefits, and state pensions form the **social safety net**. Evidence on **conditional cash transfers** — notably Brazil's Bolsa Família and Mexico's Progresa/Oportunidades (later Prospera) — shows strong effects on school attendance and child nutrition at modest fiscal cost. Banerjee and Duflo, 2019 Nobel laureates, provide randomised-trial evidence that cash transfers reduce extreme poverty without significant work disincentives.

**Universal basic income (UBI)** remains contested. The GiveDirectly trial in Kenya, running since 2017 and with interim results published through 2023 and 2024, found that monthly cash transfers (roughly 22.50 US dollars per adult per month) produced meaningful gains in food security, assets, and well-being without discouraging work. Finland's 2017–2018 trial found modest effects on well-being but minimal employment impact. These are not conclusive, but they challenge strong priors.

India's **Garib Kalyan Yojana** programme, extended through 2024, delivers free food grains to roughly 800 million beneficiaries — the world's largest food-based safety net. Evaluation should note its scale, its fiscal cost, and concerns around leakage and targeting.

### Minimum wage

A **minimum wage** sets a legal floor on hourly pay. Classical theory predicts unemployment in competitive labour markets, since the floor is set above the market-clearing wage.

The modern empirical literature, starting with Card and Krueger's 1994 New Jersey–Pennsylvania study, finds that modest minimum wages often have minimal or no employment effect. Explanations include monopsony power (employers pay below the marginal-product wage and can absorb the floor), reduced turnover costs, and efficiency-wage effects.

Pakistan, India, the UAE (for domestic workers), and Saudi Arabia all operate minimum-wage structures, though enforcement varies. The UK's National Living Wage for workers aged 21 and over rose to £11.44 in April 2024 and £12.21 in April 2025 — a level that approaches the Living Wage Foundation's voluntary benchmark.

Evaluation: a minimum wage set at a reasonable fraction of the median (typically 50–60%) tends to raise lower earnings without heavy employment cost; very high levels risk job losses and informal-sector growth.

### Education and training

Education is the most-cited long-run policy. It raises the productivity and wages of lower-income groups, narrows the skill premium, and expands opportunity.

Mechanisms:
- Public primary and secondary education, especially for girls.
- Vocational training and apprenticeships.
- Tertiary-education access through grants and loans.

India's Right to Education Act (2009) and efforts to expand vocational training under Skill India; the UAE's investment in university access through scholarships; Singapore's SkillsFuture programme are examples. Evaluation: education is a long-run policy — benefits take a generation to flow fully — and depends heavily on teaching quality, not just years of schooling, as work by Lant Pritchett and Karthik Muralidharan shows.

### Land reform

In agrarian economies, **land reform** — redistributing farmland or securing tenure — has historically been one of the most powerful anti-poverty interventions. South Korea and Taiwan's post-war reforms underpinned their rapid growth. More recent, less comprehensive reforms include Rwanda's Land Tenure Regularisation Programme, which registered millions of parcels and particularly benefited women's ownership rights.

### Financial inclusion

**Financial inclusion** — access to bank accounts, savings, credit, and insurance — allows poor households to smooth consumption, invest in businesses, and survive shocks. Kenya's M-Pesa mobile money platform, launched in 2007 and now used across East Africa, has been shown in randomised studies (Suri and Jack) to lift households out of poverty. India's Pradhan Mantri Jan Dhan Yojana has opened roughly 500 million bank accounts since 2014.

### Healthcare and public services

Universal access to primary healthcare reduces catastrophic health spending — a major cause of falling into poverty. The Lancet Commission on Investing in Health and WHO analyses consistently find primary-care investment yields high returns in both health and economic terms.

### Labour-market regulation and anti-discrimination

Paid parental leave, equal-pay laws, anti-discrimination statutes, and union rights raise the earnings of groups exposed to lower pay. Saudi Arabia's 2019–2021 reforms allowing women to drive, travel, and work without guardianship were labour-market policies as much as social ones. The UAE's 2022 labour law and 2023 cabinet gender targets sit in the same category.

### Policy package thinking

No single policy eliminates poverty. Most effective strategies combine transfers (immediate income), investment (education, health, infrastructure), and reforms (labour protection, anti-discrimination). Dani Rodrik argues developing countries should sequence policies pragmatically, protecting what works and tolerating heterodox arrangements where they fit local political economy.

## Equity vs efficiency — the classic debate

The **equity-efficiency trade-off** is one of the defining debates in welfare economics. Arthur Okun's 1975 "leaky bucket" metaphor captures it: redistribution may shrink the overall pie through disincentive effects on work, saving, and investment, even as it shares the pie more equally.

Three positions frame IAL evaluation:

### Efficiency-first

Milton Friedman and Friedrich Hayek argued that markets, absent rent-seeking, produce outcomes that reward contribution to output. Redistribution beyond a basic minimum distorts prices and weakens incentives. Growth is the most powerful anti-poverty mechanism. A negative income tax (Friedman) can guarantee a floor without removing incentives.

### Equity-first

John Rawls's "veil of ignorance" argument (1971) suggests society should be organised so inequality is permitted only insofar as it benefits the worst off (the *difference principle*). Piketty, Stiglitz, and Zucman argue high contemporary inequality threatens democracy and is not well explained by productivity differences, calling for strong redistribution.

### Pragmatic middle

Sen, Stiglitz, and IMF research (2014 and later) have argued that, within the range of policies actually on offer, better-designed redistribution can increase both equity *and* growth — by investing in health and education, reducing consumption volatility for poor households, and removing labour-market frictions. Banerjee and Duflo's work in particular treats the trade-off as empirical — to be tested case by case, not assumed.

Sen's *capability approach* reframes the debate: poverty is a failure of substantive freedoms — the real opportunities people have to live a life they value — rather than an income threshold. This shifts policy design toward health, education, voice, and participation, not only cash.

## Developing vs developed country contexts

IAL answers should adapt to the country context.

### Developed-country emphasis
- Relative poverty, social exclusion, in-work poverty.
- Tax and transfer systems doing most of the redistributive work.
- Debates focus on welfare design, minimum wage, and top-end tax rates.

### Developing-country emphasis
- Absolute poverty and deprivation remain central.
- Informal economy is vast, so direct labour-market regulation reaches only a minority.
- Infrastructure gaps — water, sanitation, electricity — depress productivity.
- Fiscal space is limited; design of targeted transfers (e.g., conditional cash transfers) matters more than tax-rate choice.
- Remittances from the GCC, the US, and Europe are major anti-poverty channels for Pakistan, Sri Lanka, Bangladesh, the Philippines, and much of sub-Saharan Africa. 2024 remittance flows to low- and middle-income countries reached roughly 685 billion US dollars on World Bank estimates.
- Climate vulnerability compounds poverty — floods in Pakistan in 2022 affected more than 33 million people and pushed millions into deeper poverty.

Regional snapshots relevant to IAL students:
- **UAE and Saudi Arabia** — high nominal income, but inequality between citizens and migrant workers is stark. Kafala-system reforms from 2020 onwards in Saudi Arabia and Qatar illustrate how labour-market rules shape inequality.
- **India** — falling consumption poverty on some estimates, but wealth inequality among the highest in large economies.
- **Pakistan and Sri Lanka** — stabilisation programmes and debt distress constraining social spending.
- **Maldives** — tourism-driven growth, with spatial inequality between the capital and outer atolls.

## Exam technique and evaluation frameworks

Unit 4 (WEC14) questions on poverty and inequality usually take one of three forms:

- **Data-response**: interpret Lorenz curves, Gini coefficients, HDI/MPI rankings. Always interpret *the direction and magnitude*, not just the number.
- **Evaluate a policy** (10–14 marks): discuss a named policy with intended effects, unintended effects, and a judgement.
- **Extended essay** (20+ marks): "Discuss whether the benefits of redistribution outweigh the costs" or "Evaluate policies to reduce poverty in a developing country of your choice."

**High-mark evaluation moves:**
- Distinguish short-run from long-run effects.
- Compare developing and developed contexts explicitly.
- Use the equity-efficiency trade-off as a structuring device.
- Cite at least one named economist (Sen, Piketty, Banerjee and Duflo, Stiglitz, Rodrik, Friedman) and one real country example, ideally post-2023.
- End with a justified conclusion — "On balance, in a lower-middle-income country with weak tax capacity, targeted conditional cash transfers are likely to deliver more poverty reduction per unit of fiscal cost than a general minimum-wage increase…"

**Diagram discipline:**
- Lorenz curves must have axes labelled "cumulative % population" and "cumulative % income".
- Show a policy shift by moving the curve closer to the line of equality.
- When referencing the Gini, state whether it is pre-tax or post-tax.

## Common mistakes

- Confusing absolute and relative poverty.
- Treating the Gini coefficient as giving information about levels of income — it does not.
- Reading Lorenz curves the wrong way round (axes swapped).
- Assuming education is a short-run solution to poverty.
- Ignoring informal-sector dynamics when proposing minimum-wage policies in developing economies.
- Treating inequality and poverty as the same thing — a society can reduce inequality without reducing poverty and vice versa.
- Over-relying on GDP per capita as a measure of living standards.
- Misusing Piketty's r > g as a universal truth rather than a contested empirical claim.
- Failing to evaluate — listing policies without discussing effectiveness or drawbacks.
- Value judgements without economic reasoning ("the rich should pay more because it is fair") rather than "progressive taxation is justified on equity grounds, subject to efficiency costs at high marginal rates".
