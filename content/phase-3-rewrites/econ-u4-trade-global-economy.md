# Trade and the Global Economy — Edexcel IAL Economics

*Unit 4 (WEC14) · Last updated: 2026-04*

## Key takeaways (60 seconds)

- **Absolute advantage** means producing more of a good with the same resources; **comparative advantage** means producing at a lower opportunity cost — and it is comparative, not absolute, advantage that drives gains from trade.
- **Terms of trade** is the ratio of export prices to import prices (× 100); an improvement means each unit of exports buys more imports, but whether that helps the economy depends on elasticities.
- **Trading blocs** range from free-trade areas (USMCA) to customs unions (Mercosur) to single markets (EU) to monetary unions (eurozone); deeper integration brings both larger gains and bigger sovereignty costs.
- **Protectionism** uses tariffs, quotas, subsidies, and non-tariff barriers (NTBs) to shield domestic producers; each distorts prices, creates a deadweight loss, but may be defended on infant-industry, strategic, or employment grounds.
- **The WTO** sets trade rules and adjudicates disputes, but its appellate body has been paralysed since 2019, weakening the multilateral trading system at exactly the moment trade tensions are rising.
- **Exchange rates** transmit directly to trade flows; a depreciation makes exports cheaper and imports dearer, but the current account effect depends on the Marshall–Lerner condition and shows the J-curve dynamic in practice.
- **Developing-country** gains from trade are real but uneven; preferential access, commodity dependence, and global value-chain positioning shape who wins.

## Why trade matters more than ever

Around 2024, world merchandise trade was roughly $25 trillion and world commercial services trade added around $7.5 trillion more. No major economy — rich, middle-income, or low-income — is self-sufficient in the sense your grandparents might have understood that term. The raw materials in a smartphone criss-cross the world before assembly; food security in the Gulf depends on imports from multiple continents; higher education in the UK and Australia has been reshaped by international student flows; and Apple's progressive shift of iPhone assembly from China to India and Vietnam between 2022 and 2024 is quietly rewiring global electronics trade.

Unit 4 asks you to handle this complexity with a few powerful tools: comparative advantage, terms of trade, trading blocs, and the effects of protection. You also have to hold in mind that trade is not just an efficiency story. It is a political story about who wins, who loses, and how governments respond. That is why post-2020 politics has brought back industrial policy, tariffs, subsidies, and trade defence in a way no A-level cohort has seen before.

## Absolute and comparative advantage

### Absolute advantage

**A country has absolute advantage when it can produce more of a good than another country using the same resources.** Adam Smith's original framing in 1776 was that trade lets each country specialise in what it produces most efficiently, raising global output. Saudi Arabia has absolute advantage in low-cost oil extraction because its reserves are accessible and extraction costs are low. Chile has absolute advantage in copper; Vietnam in labour-intensive textile assembly; Switzerland in precision watches and certain pharmaceuticals.

### Comparative advantage

**Comparative advantage is the ability to produce a good at a lower opportunity cost than another country, even if the other country has absolute advantage in both goods.** David Ricardo's 1817 insight was that trade still produces gains even when one country is better at everything. What matters is opportunity cost: what each country must give up to produce a good.

A quick numerical illustration (these numbers are purely stylised). Imagine Country A can produce either 100 units of wheat or 50 units of cloth with its resources; Country B can produce either 30 wheat or 30 cloth. Country A has absolute advantage in both. But in Country A, the opportunity cost of 1 cloth is 2 wheat; in Country B it is 1 wheat. Cloth is relatively cheaper in B. If B specialises in cloth and A in wheat, and they trade at any price between 1 and 2 wheat per cloth, both are better off than in autarky.

### Assumptions and real-world qualifications

The model assumes constant returns to scale, perfect factor mobility within countries, no transport costs, and static technology. None of these is perfectly true. Transport costs explain why some seemingly good trades never happen; skill specificity explains why displaced workers often cannot move smoothly into export sectors; and dynamic effects mean today's comparative advantage can move — as South Korea showed by climbing from textiles in the 1960s to semiconductors and shipbuilding by the 2000s. Joseph Stiglitz has argued that the standard model understates the adjustment costs borne by displaced workers and communities — a point that became politically central in the 2010s and 2020s.

Dani Rodrik's work emphasises the same adjustment problem: trade creates aggregate gains but produces identifiable losers, and whether the gains exceed the losses in welfare terms depends on how the losses are cushioned. This is why IAL evaluation increasingly expects candidates to discuss losers as well as winners.

## Terms of trade

**The terms of trade (TOT) is the ratio of export prices to import prices, usually expressed as an index: TOT = (index of export prices / index of import prices) × 100.** An improvement in the terms of trade means export prices have risen relative to import prices — each unit of exports now buys more imports.

An improvement is not automatically good. If it results from rising global demand for a country's exports (a commodity boom, for instance), the country gains unambiguously. If it results from a currency appreciation that makes exports uncompetitive on volume, the volume decline can outweigh the price gain, and the current account can worsen.

Between 2022 and 2024, energy-exporting economies — Saudi Arabia, the UAE, Qatar, Norway, Australia — saw their terms of trade improve materially as global oil and LNG prices stayed elevated relative to import prices. Pure-commodity exporters with narrow export bases (for instance, Angola or Nigeria in oil) gained too, but remained exposed to the next downturn. This illustrates the **Prebisch–Singer hypothesis**, associated with Raul Prebisch and Hans Singer, which argues that commodity exporters face secularly declining terms of trade over the long run because demand for commodities is less elastic than demand for manufactures. The 2022–2024 commodity cycle is a reminder that the long run contains short runs where prices do rise — but also that the Prebisch–Singer logic can reassert itself in the next cycle.

## Patterns of world trade

World trade has four features students should know.

First, **intra-regional trade** — trade between neighbours — is a larger share of the total than most students expect. Around half of global trade is intra-regional, particularly in Europe and in Asia. The three big trade regions (North America, Europe, Asia) trade more within themselves than across.

Second, **manufactures dominate**, accounting for close to two-thirds of world goods trade, with commodities around a quarter and agricultural products smaller still. Services trade is growing faster than goods trade, driven by digital delivery of software, consulting, and increasingly generative-AI-enabled services.

Third, **global value chains** — where production is split across many countries — mean gross trade flows overstate value added. An iPhone assembled in India and counted as a $1,000 export contains components from South Korea, Japan, Taiwan, and the US; only a fraction of that $1,000 is Indian value added. This has become more important as firms diversify supply chains post-COVID.

Fourth, trade is **more volatile than GDP**. When world GDP falls by 1% in a recession, world trade often falls by several per cent. The Red Sea shipping disruption from late 2023 into 2024, following attacks on vessels in the Bab-el-Mandeb strait, added days to voyage times and several thousand dollars per container to the Asia–Europe route. The ripple reached every goods-importing business.

## Trading blocs

Trading blocs sit on a spectrum of integration.

- **Free-trade area.** Members remove tariffs and quotas between themselves but each sets its own external tariff. USMCA (which replaced NAFTA in 2020) is the largest example. AfCFTA — the African Continental Free Trade Area — launched trading under a single framework in 2021 and continues to phase in. RCEP (Regional Comprehensive Economic Partnership), covering 15 Asia-Pacific economies including China, Japan, South Korea, Australia, and the ASEAN members, entered into force in 2022.
- **Customs union.** A free-trade area plus a common external tariff. Mercosur in South America, and the EU Customs Union, are the main examples. A customs union enables negotiation as a bloc with third countries.
- **Single market.** A customs union plus free movement of labour and capital and broadly harmonised regulation. The European Single Market (1993 onwards) is the textbook case.
- **Monetary union.** A single market plus a shared currency. The eurozone (19 members around 2024) is the archetype.

Benefits of blocs include **trade creation** — where trade shifts from higher-cost domestic production to lower-cost bloc partners — and access to scale economies. Costs include **trade diversion** — where trade shifts from a low-cost third country to a higher-cost bloc partner because of tariff differentials — and loss of policy sovereignty, particularly sharp in monetary unions where members give up independent monetary policy.

The EU's 2024 imposition of provisional additional tariffs on Chinese battery-electric vehicles illustrates how customs-union membership operates: the tariff is set at EU level, applied at every member's border, and binds each member even if some would prefer different treatment. BYD, SAIC, and Geely responded by announcing investments in European production — a classic **tariff jumping** move.

BRICS (Brazil, Russia, India, China, South Africa) expanded in 2024 to include additional members including the UAE, Egypt, Ethiopia, and Iran. BRICS is not a trading bloc in the technical sense — there is no internal tariff regime — but it reflects a shift in the pattern of trade cooperation. South–South trade has grown faster than North–South trade for over a decade.

## The WTO and the rules-based system

The World Trade Organization, established in 1995 as successor to the General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade (GATT), has three core functions: writing the rule book of international trade; providing a forum for negotiating new rules; and adjudicating disputes between members.

Two core WTO principles shape trade law. **Most-favoured-nation (MFN) treatment** requires a member that grants a tariff concession to one WTO member to grant the same concession to all others, preventing discrimination. **National treatment** requires imported goods to be treated no less favourably than domestic goods once they have cleared customs.

Since late 2019, the WTO's appellate body — the final court of its dispute settlement system — has been effectively paralysed because the US has blocked new appointments. Disputes can still be filed, but appeals can be sent "into the void," reducing predictability. The Doha Round of multilateral trade talks, launched in 2001, has stalled for more than 15 years. Rodrik has argued that the WTO's weakness reflects a deeper failure to update trade rules for services, digital trade, and climate concerns.

Alongside WTO decay, plurilateral and regional agreements have proliferated. The Comprehensive and Progressive Trans-Pacific Partnership (CPTPP), which the UK formally joined in 2024, is one example.

## Protectionism

### Tariffs

**A tariff is a tax on imported goods.** A tariff raises the domestic price of imports. Domestic producers supply more, consumers buy less, and the government collects tariff revenue. But the combined cost to consumers exceeds the combined benefits to producers, government, and any resource reallocation — creating a deadweight welfare loss.

The most visible tariff episode of the 2020s was the US–China tariff war that began in 2018 and was only partly recalibrated thereafter. By 2024, the US was weighing and imposing further tariffs on Chinese electric vehicles, batteries, and solar panels under Section 301. Economic estimates consistently suggest the bulk of the cost was borne by US importers and consumers through higher prices, with modest revenue to the US government and some relocation of production to third countries like Vietnam and Mexico — an example of **tariff-induced trade diversion**.

### Quotas

A quota is a quantitative limit on imports. Unlike a tariff, the price rise is captured by whoever holds the import quota license — often foreign exporters — rather than by the domestic government. A "voluntary export restraint" is a quota agreed with an exporter to head off more punitive action. Voluntary export restraints on Japanese cars into the US in the 1980s are the textbook case.

### Subsidies

A subsidy to domestic producers shifts their supply curve rightwards, letting them compete with imports at the market price. Subsidies avoid the direct tariff-on-consumers effect but are paid from tax revenue and can still trigger WTO disputes. The US Inflation Reduction Act (2022) and the EU Green Deal Industrial Plan rolled out large-scale production and investment subsidies for electric vehicles, batteries, and clean-energy equipment — raising disputes about whether these were compatible with WTO subsidy rules.

### Non-tariff barriers

**Non-tariff barriers (NTBs) are any restriction on trade other than a tariff or quota.** Examples include product standards, labelling requirements, licensing regimes, sanitary and phytosanitary rules, and customs procedures. NTBs are often defended on legitimate grounds (consumer safety, environmental protection) but can also act as disguised protection.

A leading example in 2024 is the EU **Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM)**. Beginning its transitional phase in late 2023 and scheduled to move to full financial effect from 2026, CBAM applies an effective carbon charge to imports of carbon-intensive goods like cement, steel, aluminium, fertilisers, electricity, and hydrogen. The EU's stated purpose is to prevent "carbon leakage" — production moving offshore to avoid EU climate rules. Developing-country exporters, including India and several African economies, have argued CBAM is a disguised trade barrier that shifts climate-adjustment costs onto them.

### Arguments for protectionism

The standard analytical arguments examiners reward are:

- **Infant industry argument.** Originally Alexander Hamilton's 1791 case for US manufacturing; developed by Friedrich List. The idea: new industries need temporary protection until they achieve scale and learning economies.
- **Strategic industries.** Defence, food, energy, and — increasingly — advanced semiconductors. The US CHIPS and Science Act (2022) and parallel EU legislation reflect strategic concerns about semiconductor supply concentration in Taiwan.
- **Protection against dumping.** If a foreign firm sells below cost to destroy domestic competition, anti-dumping duties can be defended. But "dumping" allegations are easily abused for ordinary protection.
- **Employment and transition costs.** Sudden import surges can destroy local employment faster than workers can retrain.
- **National security and resilience.** Post-COVID and post-Ukraine, governments place a premium on supply-chain resilience even at a cost to pure efficiency.

### Arguments against protectionism

- **Higher prices for consumers.** The most immediate and largest effect.
- **Deadweight welfare loss.** Resources misallocated away from comparative advantage.
- **Retaliation.** Trade wars often escalate through tit-for-tat responses, hurting all participants. The 2018–2020 US–China episode is the canonical case.
- **X-inefficiency.** Sheltered producers lose competitive pressure to improve.
- **Long-run growth.** Open economies historically grow faster than closed ones — though causation runs in both directions.

## Exchange rates and trade

Exchange rates link trade flows to monetary economics. A depreciation of the currency (the home currency buys less foreign currency) makes exports cheaper in foreign currency terms and imports dearer in home-currency terms. Intuitively, this should improve the current account.

In practice, the short-run effect is often the opposite — the **J-curve effect**. Immediately after a depreciation, import volumes have not yet adjusted (contracts are in place, supply chains take time to reroute), so paying more for the same import volume worsens the current account. Over time, as price signals move volumes, exports rise and imports fall, and the current account improves. The time path traces out a J shape.

See Diagram: `j-curve-exchange-rate.svg` — current account deteriorates immediately after depreciation, then improves over time as volumes respond.

Whether a depreciation eventually improves the current account at all depends on the **Marshall–Lerner condition**: the sum of the absolute price elasticities of demand for exports and imports must exceed 1. If elasticities are too low (for instance, if a country's exports are specialised commodities with few substitutes, or its imports are essential capital goods with no domestic alternative), a depreciation may not help and can even hurt.

Pakistan's rupee depreciation during its 2022–2024 external financing stresses made imported fuel and food sharply more expensive, with limited short-run improvement in the current account because commodity imports were not easily reduced. Turkey's extended currency slide over the 2020s shows a similar pattern — import inflation hit households before the current account rebalanced.

## Current events framing: what to have in your head for exams

The 2023–2026 period has given you a rich backdrop. The following are specific, recent developments you can cite credibly, with stakes drawn clearly:

- **US–China semiconductor controls (ongoing).** US export restrictions on advanced chips and chip-making equipment to China, with the Netherlands (ASML) and Japan (Tokyo Electron) joining. A non-tariff barrier on technology with enormous implications for global value chains.
- **Red Sea shipping disruption (late 2023 into 2024).** Houthi attacks on commercial vessels forced major carriers to route around the Cape of Good Hope, adding roughly 10 days and thousands of dollars per container to Asia–Europe voyages. A live illustration of how logistical shocks transmit through trade.
- **EU provisional tariffs on Chinese electric vehicles (2024).** EU anti-subsidy duties on imported Chinese BEVs, ranging up to around 35% on top of the existing duty depending on the producer. BYD, SAIC, and others responded with plans for European production.
- **Shein and Temu entry into the US and EU markets.** Ultra-low-price, cross-border e-commerce from China exploiting the **de minimis rule** (packages below a threshold value entering duty-free) has shaken conventional retail and triggered regulatory responses.
- **Apple's India shift.** By 2024, Apple was producing a meaningful share of iPhones in India, partnering with Foxconn and Tata Electronics. This diversifies away from China and has macroeconomic implications for India's export growth.
- **EU CBAM transitional phase.** Reporting obligations from late 2023; full financial mechanism from 2026. A landmark case for climate-based NTBs.
- **BRICS expansion (January 2024).** New members include the UAE, Egypt, Ethiopia, and Iran, extending the grouping's economic weight and giving it additional oil-producing members.
- **Ozempic and GLP-1 drug supply chains.** Global shortages of semaglutide-based drugs in 2023–2024 highlighted concentration in pharmaceutical production and prompted new capacity investment in Denmark and the US.
- **BYD's European push.** Beyond responding to tariffs, BYD's planned Hungarian plant (announced in 2023) illustrates how emerging-market multinationals are internationalising production.

## Developed versus developing economies

### Gains and losses

Developed economies tend to benefit from access to lower-cost inputs, export markets, and services exports. Consumers in rich countries gain from cheap imports; exporting firms gain from larger addressable markets; displaced workers lose in specific sectors where imports crowd out domestic production.

Developing economies potentially gain the most: access to rich-country markets, foreign direct investment, technology transfer, scale effects, and movement up the value chain. South Korea, Taiwan, Singapore, and more recently Vietnam illustrate what export-led industrialisation can deliver. But the gains are unevenly distributed.

### The development-specific challenges

- **Commodity dependence.** Many low-income economies remain dependent on a few commodity exports, exposing them to Prebisch–Singer effects and to volatile global prices. Zambia and copper; Bangladesh and ready-made garments; Côte d'Ivoire and cocoa — each is a case where the terms of trade are determined by a narrow external market.
- **Global value-chain positioning.** Value added captured by developing countries in a typical electronics or textile value chain is often a small share of the final price. Moving up the chain — from assembly into design or branded production — is what delivers real development gains.
- **Tariff escalation.** Many rich-country tariff schedules rise with the level of processing — low duties on raw coffee beans, higher on roasted beans, higher still on branded coffee. This discourages developing-country producers from moving up the value chain.
- **Non-tariff barriers.** Compliance costs for product standards, sanitary rules, and increasingly environmental requirements (like CBAM) fall disproportionately on smaller, poorer producers.
- **Aid for trade and preferential access.** Schemes like the EU's Everything But Arms initiative and the US African Growth and Opportunity Act (AGOA) have provided preferential market access for LDCs, but compliance costs and rules of origin mean take-up has been uneven.

Rodrik's argument that trade policy should be tailored to each country's stage of development is especially relevant here. A policy framework that works for a high-income service exporter may not fit a middle-income manufacturing exporter or a low-income commodity exporter.

## Exam technique and evaluation frameworks

Unit 4 trade questions almost always reward the following moves:

1. **Use comparative advantage as the baseline.** Even in a question about protection, start by naming the source of gains from trade.
2. **Distinguish short-run and long-run effects.** A tariff has an immediate price effect and a slower volume effect. A currency depreciation has a perverse short-run effect before the J-curve kicks in.
3. **Apply the elasticity frame.** The Marshall–Lerner condition matters for exchange rates. Elasticity of demand for a country's exports matters for terms-of-trade analysis.
4. **Distinguish developed and developing perspectives.** Many mark schemes explicitly reward candidates who consider both. Avoid assuming Western conditions apply everywhere.
5. **Anchor in a specific, recent example.** Examiners reward contextual knowledge. Red Sea, CBAM, Apple-in-India, EU–China EV tariffs are all fair game for WEC14 in 2026.

Common command-word traps:

- "Discuss" — examiners want a balanced argument, not a list.
- "Examine" — requires depth in a single argument, not breadth.
- "Assess" — requires judgement, like "evaluate."
- "To what extent" — explicitly demands a position.

Mark-scheme signals include: correct use of "comparative" vs "absolute" advantage; correct identification of trade creation vs trade diversion; diagrams for tariff effects with deadweight loss triangle labelled; a specific named example; and a developing-country perspective on any question about protection.

## Common mistakes

- **Using "absolute advantage" when you mean "comparative advantage."** The whole point of Ricardo is that comparative advantage drives trade even when absolute advantage sits on one side. This is the single most common marker penalty.
- **Treating an improvement in the terms of trade as automatically good.** It can help or hurt depending on elasticities and the cause of the change.
- **Confusing trade creation and trade diversion.** Creation = efficiency gain; diversion = efficiency loss. Both can occur within the same bloc, and the net welfare effect depends on which dominates.
- **Ignoring non-tariff barriers.** Students default to tariffs and quotas. In 2024, NTBs like CBAM, product standards, and export controls are often more consequential than any tariff.
- **Treating protection as always bad or always good.** A high-level answer acknowledges the infant-industry and strategic-industries cases while recognising the deadweight losses.
- **Forgetting the developing-country angle.** Mark schemes consistently reward candidates who show they have thought about trade from the perspective of lower-income exporters.
- **Assuming the Marshall–Lerner condition always holds.** Do not confuse the conclusion of a depreciation helping the current account with the condition that makes it possible.

Use these before, during, and after reading:

- **During — explain:** Explain in your own words, using a numerical example, why two countries can gain from trade even if one is better at producing both goods.
